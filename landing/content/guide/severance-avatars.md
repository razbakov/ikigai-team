# Severance, but for AI

Today I had a useful framing for Ikigai Team.

If you have seen *Severance*, you already understand the core idea.

In the show, people at work are split from people at home. Their work self does not remember their personal life. Their personal self does not remember their work. It is a brutal version of context separation.

Ikigai Team probably needs a softer, healthier version of the same thing.

## One person, different avatars

When you set up Ikigai Team for yourself, the first thing you should get is not a random swarm of agents.

You should get an **avatar**.

That avatar is the identity boundary for a specific part of your life.

For example:

- a **personal avatar** that knows your goals, relationships, health context, values, routines, and long-term life direction
- a **work avatar** that knows your role, team, projects, deadlines, meetings, organizational context, and professional priorities

These should not automatically share memory.

Your work avatar should not casually know your private reflections.
Your personal avatar should not constantly drag work context into your life.

That separation is not just a privacy feature. It is a cognitive feature.

It helps the system stay clear.
It helps the human feel safe.
It helps the assistant act with the right priorities.

## Agents are not the identity

This also clarified something else for me.

The main product is not really "a team of six agents."
The main product is **contextual identity separation**.

Agents come after that.

Once the avatar exists, then the agent team forms around it:

- **Sage** for reflection, coaching, meaning
- **Marco** for strategy and tradeoffs
- **Maya** for operations, planning, delegation
- **Viktor** for engineering and technical execution
- **Luna** for content and growth
- **Kai** for community and partnerships

These agents are easier to remember because they have names, faces, voices, and personalities.

Humans route by character.
If I need life advice, I go to Sage.
If I need delegation and planning, I go to Maya.
If I need technical depth, I go to Viktor.

That is much more memorable than one generic assistant with a giant hidden prompt.

## Same agent, different scope

The interesting part is that agents can stay conceptually the same while changing their scope.

For example:

- personal Maya
- work Maya
- organization Maya
- project Maya

Same role.
Different context boundary.
Different memory.
Different priorities.

That feels cleaner than inventing a brand new cast of characters for every project.

## What about organizations?

The next circle beyond the personal avatar is the organization.

An organization is not just a folder or a shared drive.
It is a context where multiple humans share goals, constraints, responsibilities, and language.

That probably means an organization needs its own avatar too.

Not a human-like identity in the same way as a personal avatar, but a clear system identity with:

- shared goals
- principles
- operating rules
- domain knowledge
- memory boundaries
- access boundaries

Then projects can sit inside the organization as more focused execution contexts.

A project may not need a fully separate permanent team of agents, but it probably does need scoped context:

- codebase
- vision
- user stories
- jobs to be done
- architecture decisions
- delivery status

## Should the avatars ever talk?

This is where it gets really interesting.

In *Severance*, the split is absolute.
That is what makes it horrifying.

For an AI system, maybe the split should be the default, but not absolute.

Maybe personal and work avatars should have a **controlled communication channel**.

Not full memory merge.
Not silent leaking.
Not permanent background sharing.

A deliberate consultation mechanism.

For example:

- "My work avatar thinks I should take this promotion"
- "My personal avatar is worried this will destroy my energy and relationships"
- "Let them debate and help me see the tradeoff"

This could be one of the most valuable features in the whole system.

Because many important decisions are cross-boundary decisions:

- work-life balance
- taking a new job
- moving cities for career reasons
- starting a company
- saying no to a project that pays well but drains meaning

A single assistant often flattens these tensions.
Two avatars with different responsibilities might make the tension visible.

That is useful.

## The deeper model

Right now, the architecture feels something like this:

- **Avatar** = who this system is for, and what boundary of life it represents
- **Agents** = specialized voices and roles around that avatar
- **Organization** = shared mission and operational context for multiple people
- **Project** = focused execution context
- **Skills / frameworks** = the methods each agent can use

That means:

- agents are the **who**
- skills are the **how**
- avatar, organization, and project are the **where**
- memory and goals are the **why**

This is still early, but it feels more true than "here are six agents, have fun."

## Why this matters

People do not just want AI that is smart.
They want AI that knows which version of them it is talking to.

The self at work is not the same as the self at home.
The self planning a quarter is not the same as the self having a hard evening.
The self running a company is not the same as the self wondering what actually matters.

If AI is going to become a real collaborator, it needs boundaries, not just memory.

Maybe that is the real starting point for Ikigai Team.

Not a list of agents.
Not a setup wizard.
Not a gallery of skills.

First: create the right avatar.
Then: give it the right team.
Then: let those teams talk only when it truly helps.
